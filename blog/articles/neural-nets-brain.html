AI & Neural Networks

How Neural Networks Are Learning From the Brain They're Named After

When Frank Rosenblatt built the Perceptron in 1958, he was explicitly trying to model how biological neurons process information. Seven decades later, the relationship between artificial and biological neural networks has come full circle — with modern AI researchers increasingly turning back to neuroscience for inspiration.

The Attention Connection

The transformer architecture, which powers every major large language model from GPT to Claude, is built around a mechanism called "attention" — the ability to selectively focus computational resources on the most relevant parts of an input. Research published in Nature Neuroscience has demonstrated that this mechanism mirrors how the brain's prefrontal cortex allocates cognitive resources across competing information streams.

This parallel is more than metaphorical. Both systems face the same fundamental challenge: how to process vast amounts of information efficiently when computational resources are finite. The brain solves this through selective attention and neural gating; transformers solve it through learned attention weights. The convergence suggests that there may be deep computational principles that any intelligent system — biological or artificial — must implement.

Neuromorphic Computing

While transformers borrow conceptual principles from the brain, neuromorphic chips attempt to replicate the brain's actual computational substrate. Unlike traditional processors that separate memory and computation, neuromorphic chips process information where it is stored — just as neurons do.

The results are dramatic: Intel's Loihi 2 neuromorphic processor achieves certain pattern recognition tasks using 100 times less energy than conventional chips. For edge computing, autonomous vehicles, and always-on sensing applications, this energy efficiency advantage is transformative.

The Bidirectional Future

The most exciting development may be how AI is now being used to understand the brain itself. Deep learning models trained to predict neural activity in visual cortex have revealed organizational principles that neuroscientists had never observed. AI is becoming an indispensable tool for neuroscience research, creating a positive feedback loop where brain science inspires AI, and AI illuminates brain science.

This convergence is not just academically interesting — it has profound practical implications. Better understanding of biological intelligence will lead to more capable and efficient AI systems. And AI-powered tools are accelerating our ability to decode, stimulate, and repair the brain. At the intersection of these two fields lies some of the most important science and technology of the 21st century.
